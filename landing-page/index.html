The Problem

When you first encounter a phishing email, a malware infection, an application vulnerability, a suspicious login, insider abuse, or another security incident, your security team may create a Slack channel, or a Google Doc, or a JIRA ticket, to investigate it.


But these tools fail to tame the incident's complexity as its scope grows, more people get involved, and the amount of work rises.

Following chat channels is tedious

Some teams coordinate investigations solely through chat channels. To stay up to date on large investigations, you have to read every chat message, though most messages are not relevant to you.

Existing tools don't understand investigations are tree-like

A piece of malware may spawn a sweep, which may find another piece of malware, which may spawn another sweep, and so on.

But existing ticketing systems, like TheHive and JIRA, don't let you create subtickets of subtickets. Tickets in INCIDENTS can have any number of levels of subtickets.

Investigators fear missing things

Whether you're leading a large investigation or conducting a live response, there's many leads to follow. You almost certainly will miss one unless you record them somehow.

By explicitly marking tickets as leads in INCIDENTS, you can ensure you investigate each one.

Too much unneeded communication

Investigators spend a lot of time writing status reports, on update calls, and communicating with management.

Often, stakeholders just want a high-level overview of how an investigation is progressing. They can gain such an overview by watching an investigation's tree in INCIDENTS.

Writing a report requires digging for information about the investigation

When writing a report, you may need to explain why a decision was made or why a lead was/wasn't followed. Usually, you have to ask someone, search the chat, or try to remember.

Instead, you can time-travel through an investigation in INCIDENTS (soon) to see how it developed over time.

Multiplexing people onto work is difficult

In INCIDENTS, by dividing large tasks into smaller tickets that you assign to people, you enable multiple people to work in large tasks in parallel, preventing these tasks from becoming bottlenecks.

In addition, a person can find an open ticket to work on -- he doesn't need to wait for the investigation lead to find work for them.

Our Solution

In INCIDENTS, incidents are trees of tickets, where some tickets are leads.

  • Found multiple threat actors in an environment? Create a ticket for each threat actor (under the root) and assign each threat actor to someone to investigate.
  • Or you can create a root ticket for each involved team: forensics, remediation, IT, helpdesk, legal, etc. Each team can have visibility into other teams' work while working in parallel.
  • Drag and drop nodes to organize your tree.
